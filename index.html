What is 5G?

https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/mckinsey-explainers/what-is-5g October 7, 2022

The Fourth Industrial Revolution is driven by 5G, or fifth-generation wireless technology. It's the case that the fifth time is a charm. Yes, 5G is more rapid than 4G. However, 5G offers much more than just (much) faster connectivity over its predecessors. It also offers much more stable and secure connectivity. Additionally, 5G allows data to travel with a much reduced latency—the time between data submission and arrival—between one location and another. This is how it operates: The service area of 5G networks is segmented into geographical sub-areas known as cells, just like that of all cellular networks. Every cell is equipped with local antennae that allow radio waves to connect all wireless devices within the cell to the internet and phone network.

New 3D printing method designed by Stanford engineers promises faster printing with multiple materials

https://news.stanford.edu/2022/09/28/new-3d-printer-promises-faster-multi-material-creations/ September 28, 2022

A 3D printing technique called iCLIP, created by researchers at Stanford, promises to produce prints faster by combining different types of resin into a single object. The new design, which was published in Science Advances, can print high-resolution images five to ten times faster than the fastest method currently in use. This could open the door for researchers to use thicker resins with improved mechanical and electrical properties. The object is pulled from a thin pool of resin by a rising platform in the continuous liquid interface production (CLIP) method; this new method improves upon it. The dead zone is critical to CLIP's speed; however, with iCLIP, researchers have installed syringe pumps to supplement resin at strategic locations atop the rising platform. When the object is finished or integrated into the design, the conduits that carry the resin can be removed. The conduits are printed concurrently with the design. As the printing process progresses, iCLIP offers the possibility to print using several types of resin; all that is needed is a separate syringe for each new resin. With software that optimises the fluid distribution network design for every printed piece, the researchers can potentially expedite the printing process while giving designers fine control over the boundaries between different types of resin.
